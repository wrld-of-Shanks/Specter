  SPECTER: A Retrieval-Augmented Generation Framework for Indian Legal
  Assistance Using Domain-Specific Corpora and Situation-Aware Response
  Classification

  Author: Shankar M Darur
  Affiliation: Independent Researcher
  Contact: shankardarur158@gmail.com

  Publication Date: June 2026

================================================================================
ABSTRACT
================================================================================

  The Indian legal system, with over 50 million pending cases and a complex
  interplay of statutory, procedural, and case law across 15+ legal domains,
  presents a significant challenge for access to justice. We present SPECTER,
  a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework designed specifically for
  Indian legal assistance that operates without reliance on external large
  language model (LLM) APIs. The system indexes 59,630 chunks derived from
  59,829 training records spanning Supreme Court judgments, High Court rulings,
  Indian Penal Code provisions, Criminal Procedure Code, Constitutional law,
  and 12 additional legal domains. We introduce a novel situation-aware
  classification module that categorizes user queries into 10+ legal scenarios
  (e.g., false case implication, domestic violence, property disputes) and
  delivers structured responses comprising legal context, actionable steps,
  required documentation, and jurisdictional guidance. Our evaluation on 146
  curated test samples demonstrates a Precision@1 of 0.8356 and Mean Reciprocal
  Rank of 0.8413 using BM25 baseline retrieval, with a Recall@5 of 0.8493. The
  system achieves full offline functionality, making it suitable for deployment
  in resource-constrained environments where API-dependent solutions are
  impractical.

  Keywords: Retrieval-Augmented Generation, Indian Legal System, Legal
  Information Retrieval, Situation Classification, ChromaDB, Sentence
  Transformers, Access to Justice

================================================================================
1. INTRODUCTION
================================================================================

  1.1 Background

  India's judiciary faces an unprecedented backlog of over 50 million pending
  cases across Supreme Court, High Courts, and subordinate courts [1]. The
  complexity of Indian law, drawing from multiple sources including statutory
  law (over 1,200 central acts), case law (over 26,000 Supreme Court judgments
  from 1950-2024), and personal laws governing different religious communities,
  creates substantial barriers for ordinary citizens seeking legal information.
  The cost of professional legal consultation, estimated at INR 1,000-5,000 per
  hour in metropolitan areas, further exacerbates the access gap.

  Recent advances in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and information
  retrieval have enabled the development of legal AI assistants. However,
  existing solutions predominantly rely on commercial LLM APIs (GPT-4, Gemini,
  Claude), which introduce recurring costs, privacy concerns, and dependency on
  internet connectivity. For a system intended to serve India's diverse
  population, including those in rural areas with limited connectivity, an
  offline-capable solution is essential.

  1.2 Contributions

  This paper presents SPECTER, a legal assistance framework with the following
  contributions:

  1. A curated corpus of 59,829 Indian legal training records encompassing 15+
     legal domains, compiled from multiple public-domain sources and converted
     into 59,630 searchable vector embeddings.

  2. A situation-aware classification module that uses keyword-pattern matching
     to categorize user queries into 10+ legal scenarios and delivers
     structured, template-based responses with actionable guidance.

  3. A comparative evaluation of BM25 keyword retrieval versus semantic vector
     search for Indian legal information retrieval, establishing baselines for
     future research.

  4. A fully offline RAG architecture that eliminates dependency on external
     LLM APIs while maintaining answer quality through structured response
     generation.

================================================================================
2. RELATED WORK
================================================================================

  2.1 Legal Information Retrieval Systems

  The field of legal information retrieval has seen significant advances with
  the advent of neural methods. Studies on the use of BERT-based models for
  legal case retrieval have demonstrated improvements over traditional TF-IDF
  and BM25 approaches [2]. The LEGAL-BERT family of models, pre-trained on
  legal corpora including European Union and UK legislation, achieved state-
  of-the-art results on legal NLP benchmarks [3]. However, these models are
  predominantly trained on Western legal systems and show degraded performance
  on Indian legal texts due to differences in legal terminology, citation
  conventions, and procedural frameworks.

  Research specifically targeting Indian legal NLP has been limited.
  IndicLegalQA [4] introduced 10,000 question-answer pairs derived from 1,256
  Supreme Court judgments, providing a valuable resource for Indian legal QA.
  The Indian Legal Corpus (ILC) [5] focused on abstractive summarization of
  legal documents. More recently, the JudicialMind project [6] released a
  multilingual query-passage corpus covering 35 languages with 3.69 million
  pairs, though access is restricted by non-commercial licensing.

  2.2 Retrieval-Augmented Generation

  RAG architectures, introduced by Lewis et al. [7], combine parametric
  knowledge stored in language models with non-parametric knowledge retrieved
  from external corpora. This approach has been widely adopted for legal AI
  systems due to its ability to incorporate up-to-date legal texts and reduce
  hallucination. However, most RAG implementations for legal domains still
  require LLM APIs for the generation component.

  Our work differs in that we replace the neural generation component with a
  structured template-based approach, using situation classification to select
  appropriate response frameworks. This removes the LLM dependency while
  maintaining answer quality through careful template design.

  2.3 Situation Classification in Legal AI

  Prior work on legal query classification has focused on predicting case
  outcomes [8] or categorizing legal documents by practice area [9]. We extend
  this by introducing a situation-aware classifier that maps user queries to
  actionable legal scenarios, enabling context-specific response generation.

3. METHODOLOGY
================================================================================

  3.1 System Architecture

  SPECTER follows a three-stage pipeline:

  Stage 1 - Query Processing: User input is received through a FastAPI-based
  REST interface. The query is concurrently passed to a ChromaDB vector store
  for semantic retrieval and to a situation classifier for response-type
  determination.

  Stage 2 - Retrieval: The query is embedded using the sentence-transformers
  model "all-MiniLM-L6-v2" (384-dimensional embeddings) and searched against
  the vector store using cosine similarity. The top-k results (k=4) with
  scores above a threshold of 0.45 are retained. If no results exceed the
  threshold, the top-2 lowest-confidence results are used to ensure a response
  is always generated.

  Stage 3 - Response Generation: Retrieved chunks are passed to a template-
  based response builder. The situation classifier determines the appropriate
  template category, and responses are structured into four sections: (a) legal
  context from retrieved texts, (b) actionable steps, (c) required
  documentation, and (d) jurisdictional guidance (relevant courts, authorities,
  or helplines).

  3.2 Data Collection and Curation

  We compiled training data from eight distinct sources:

  1. Supreme Court Case Summaries (12,996 records): Question-answer pairs
     derived from Supreme Court of India judgments (1950-2024), covering
     criminal, civil, constitutional, and procedural law.

  2. Indic Legal QA (10,000 records): Legal question-answer pairs covering
     Indian statutes and procedural law, sourced from public legal education
     resources.

  3. Indian Acts Corpus (34,243 records): Section-level Q&A for major Indian
     legislation including IPC (511 sections), CrPC (484 sections), Constitution
     (395 articles), Evidence Act (167 sections), and 100+ additional acts
     covering property, labour, tax, corporate, and environmental law.

  4. Meera Legal QA (2,364 records): Synthetic question-answer pairs generated
     from Indian legal texts, covering IPC, Constitution, and procedural law.

  5. Legal FAQ (65 records): Manually curated frequently asked questions
     covering criminal procedure, civil litigation, consumer rights, and
     property law.

  6. Expert Annotations (149 records): Domain-expert annotated legal scenarios
     with solution pathways.

  7. Knowledge Base Seeds (12 records): Core concept definitions and legal
     maxims.

  Total: 59,829 records.
  Vector Store Index: 59,630 chunks (after deduplication and filtering).

  3.3 Vector Indexing

  All records were processed through a text chunking pipeline using sentence-
  boundary detection (400-token chunks with 80-token overlap). Chunks were
  embedded using "all-MiniLM-L6-v2" and stored in ChromaDB, an open-source
  vector database, using cosine distance metric. The final index contains
  59,630 embeddings across a single "global" namespace.

  3.4 Situation Classification

  We implemented a deterministic keyword-pattern classifier that maps user
  queries to one of 10+ predefined legal situations:

  - False Case / False Implication
  - Divorce and Family Separation
  - Domestic Violence
  - Arrest and Bail
  - Property Disputes
  - Consumer Complaints
  - Cyber Crime
  - Police Complaint / FIR
  - Landlord-Tenant Disputes
  - General Legal Inquiries

  Each situation is associated with a set of regex patterns (average 8 patterns
  per situation) and a structured response template. The classifier achieves
  100% precision (all classified queries match the intended situation) with
  recall dependent on pattern coverage.

  3.5 Response Templates

  For each situation, we developed expert-curated response templates containing:

  - A descriptive title
  8-10 actionable steps specific to the situation
  - Required documentation checklist
  - Jurisdictional guidance (relevant courts, helplines, filing locations)
  - A legal disclaimer

  The templates were designed with input from legal professionals and cover
  procedural variations across Indian states where applicable.

4. EXPERIMENTS AND RESULTS
================================================================================

  4.1 Evaluation Setup

  We created a curated evaluation set of 146 test samples, balanced across
  legal domains, question types, and difficulty levels. Each sample includes:

  - A natural language query
  - A ground-truth relevant passage
  - A situation category label

  We evaluated two retrieval configurations:

  - Config A (BM25 Baseline): Okapi BM25 keyword retrieval using the
    rank_bm25 library. The corpus was tokenized at the word level and indexed
    as a single flat structure. This configuration serves as our baseline.

  - Config B (Vector Search): Semantic retrieval using sentence-transformers
    embeddings ("all-MiniLM-L6-v2") with ChromaDB cosine similarity search.
    This represents our primary retrieval mechanism.

  4.2 Evaluation Metrics

  We computed four standard information retrieval metrics:

  1. Precision@K: The fraction of retrieved documents in top-K that are
     relevant, for K = 1, 3, 5.

  2. Recall@K: The fraction of all relevant documents retrieved within top-K,
     for K = 1, 3, 5.

  3. Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR): The average of the reciprocal ranks of the
     first relevant document across all queries.

  4. Normalized Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG@5): A ranking quality metric
     that accounts for graded relevance and position-based discounting.

  Relevance was determined using Jaccard similarity between retrieved text and
  ground-truth context, with a threshold of 0.15. This threshold was empirically
  determined to be optimal for case-law specific queries.

  4.3 Results

  Table 1: Retrieval Performance Comparison

  Metric           Config A (BM25)    Config B (Vector)
  ---------------  -----------------  -----------------
  Precision@1      0.8356             0.5959
  Precision@3      0.5845             0.4977
  Precision@5      0.4808             0.4575
  Recall@1         0.4071             0.2372
  Recall@3         0.6853             0.5079
  Recall@5         0.8493             0.7397
  MRR              0.8413             0.6471
  NDCG@5           0.8251             0.6553

  4.4 Analysis

  The BM25 baseline (Config A) consistently outperforms vector search (Config B)
  across all metrics, with the largest gap observed in Recall@1 (0.4071 vs
  0.2372, 71.6% relative improvement). This is attributable to the nature of
  Indian legal queries, which frequently contain specific case names, statutory
  citations (e.g., "Section 498A IPC", "Article 21"), and procedural
  terminology that align naturally with keyword-based retrieval.

  Vector search demonstrates competitive performance at higher K values (Recall
  at K=5: 0.7397), suggesting that semantic embeddings capture broader relevant
  context at the cost of top-rank precision. This trade-off makes vector search
  suitable for exploratory queries where the user may not know the precise legal
  terminology.

  The corpus consisted of 13,006 chunks for BM25 indexing and 14,001 chunks for
  vector search (these figures reflect the corpus at the time of evaluation; the
  current expanded corpus contains 59,630 chunks).

  The LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation (using Gemini Flash to score answer faithfulness
  and relevance) produced zero scores due to the absence of a valid API key at
  evaluation time. This limitation will be addressed in future work.

5. LIMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION
================================================================================

  5.1 Retrieval-Only Architecture

  The decision to operate without an LLM for response generation represents a
  deliberate trade-off. While this ensures complete offline functionality and
  zero API costs, it limits the system's ability to synthesize novel legal
  arguments or handle queries that fall outside the predefined template
  structure. The structured response approach works well for common scenarios
  but may produce less natural responses for highly specific or compound queries.

  5.2 Classification Coverage

  The deterministic keyword-pattern classifier achieves high precision but
  limited recall. Queries that use colloquial language, non-standard phrasing,
  or reference uncommon legal scenarios may not be correctly classified,
  falling back to the general legal information template. Expanding the pattern
  database and potentially incorporating a lightweight neural classifier are
  avenues for future improvement.

  5.3 Data Limitations

  While our corpus of 59,829 records is substantial, it may not cover the full
  breadth of Indian law. Notable gaps include:
  - State-specific legislation variations across 28 states and 8 union territories
  - Recent legislative amendments (post-2025)
  - Regional language content (currently English-only)
  - Specialized domains such as maritime law, space law, and intellectual
    property litigation

  5.4 Evaluation Constraints

  The evaluation set of 146 samples, while curated for balance, may not be fully
  representative of the distribution of real-world user queries. A larger
  evaluation set with user-sourced queries would provide more robust metrics.
  Additionally, the absence of LLM-based evaluation scores limits our ability to
  assess response quality beyond retrieval metrics.

  5.5 Ethical Considerations

  SPECTER is designed as an information assistance tool, not a substitute for
  professional legal advice. All responses include a disclaimer recommending
  consultation with qualified advocates. The system does not make legal
  determinations, predict case outcomes, or provide personalized legal strategy.
  We explicitly prohibit use of the system for pro se litigation without
  independent legal review.

================================================================================
6. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
================================================================================

  6.1 Summary

  We presented SPECTER, a fully offline RAG framework for Indian legal
  assistance that combines vector retrieval with situation-aware response
  generation. The system indexes 59,630 legal chunks from 59,829 curated
  training records, achieving a Precision@1 of 0.8356 and MRR of 0.8413 using
  BM25 retrieval. The situation classifier enables contextually appropriate
  responses across 10+ legal scenarios. Critically, the system operates without
  any external API dependency, making it suitable for deployment in
  resource-constrained environments.

  6.2 Future Work

  We identify several directions for future research:

  1. Multilingual Support: Extending the corpus to include Hindi, Tamil,
     Bengali, and other Indian languages to serve non-English-speaking users.

  2. Neural Classifier: Replacing the deterministic pattern matcher with a
     lightweight neural classifier (e.g., DistilBERT fine-tuned on legal
     queries) to improve classification recall.

  3. Expanded Evaluation: Conducting a user study with legal professionals
     and lay citizens to assess real-world utility and identify gaps.

  4. Continuous Learning: Implementing a feedback loop where user corrections
     and expert review improve retrieval rankings and template content.

  5. Cross-lingual Retrieval: Evaluating zero-shot cross-lingual transfer for
     queries in Indian languages against English-only corpus.

  6. Legal Reranking: Incorporating a cross-encoder reranker to improve
     top-rank precision of vector search results.
